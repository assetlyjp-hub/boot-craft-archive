If I could only keep one pair of boots, it would be a chukka. Not a service boot, not a Wolverine 1000 Mile, not even my Iron Rangers. A chukka. They are the most versatile silhouette in menswear — quiet enough to disappear under wool trousers, casual enough to wear with raw denim and a tee, and dressy enough to pass at most offices that still pretend to have a dress code.

I have owned roughly a dozen chukkas over the last fifteen years. Some were brilliant. A couple were disasters. This guide is what I wish someone had handed me when I bought my first pair of Clarks Desert Boots in college and assumed all chukkas were the same thing.

## What Makes a Chukka a Chukka

The defining features are simple and rigid:

- **Two or three pairs of eyelets.** No more, no less. Four eyelets and you have crossed into derby boot territory.
- **Open lacing** (the quarters sit on top of the vamp, like a derby).
- **Ankle-height shaft**, usually finishing just above the ankle bone.
- **Plain toe**, almost always. A cap-toe chukka exists but is unusual.

The name comes from the British military in India and a polo period of play, also called a "chukker." The Clarks Desert Boot, introduced in 1949 by Nathan Clark, was inspired by boots he saw British officers wearing in Cairo. That single shoe is why most non-shoe-people picture a sand-coloured suede ankle boot when they hear the word chukka.

Worth knowing: a "desert boot" specifically refers to the crepe-soled, two-eyelet, suede version Clarks popularised. All desert boots are chukkas; not all chukkas are desert boots.

## Suede vs Leather: The Real Tradeoff

This is the first decision and most people get it wrong.

**Suede chukkas** are softer in feeling, dressier than people assume, and surprisingly resilient if you brush them weekly. They handle minor scuffs with a quick brush. They look terrible in heavy rain. Snuff suede in particular has a slight warmth to it that calf leather cannot replicate.

**Leather chukkas** — typically calf or chromexcel — are more weather-friendly, easier to wipe clean, and can be dressed up or down depending on finish. A polished calf chukka in dark brown is essentially a desk-friendly dress shoe. A chromexcel chukka in a rough natural finish reads as casual and rugged.

My honest split: if you live somewhere wet (London, Seattle, Tokyo's rainy season), start with leather. If you live somewhere with proper seasons or a dry climate, suede is the more interesting purchase. I have written more about [keeping suede looking right](/articles/how-to-clean-suede-boots) and [what regular leather care actually looks like](/articles/how-to-care-for-leather-boots) — both matter for chukkas because the small surface area shows neglect quickly.

## When a Chukka Actually Works

People love claiming chukkas "go with everything." They do not. They go with quite a lot, but not literally everything.

**Smart casual:** This is the sweet spot. Chinos, dark denim, wool trousers, oxford cloth button-down shirts, knit polos. Suede chukkas in sand or snuff are bulletproof here.

**Business casual:** A dark calf chukka — think Crockett & Jones Tetbury in dark brown — passes at most offices that have moved away from full suiting. Pair with grey wool trousers and a navy sport coat and nobody will think twice.

**Full suit:** Honestly, no. A chukka under a proper two-piece suit looks like a compromise. If the suit is unstructured Italian cotton or a soft tweed, you can almost get away with it, but a polished cap-toe oxford is the right answer.

**Heavy workwear or rugged outdoor looks:** Also no. The shaft is too short and the silhouette too elegant. Reach for a moc toe or a service boot.

**Shorts:** Do not do this. I know people on Instagram do this. They are wrong.

## Tier-by-Tier Picks

### Entry — Clarks Desert Boot ($110–$140)

The original. Two eyelets, crepe sole, suede upper. Made in Vietnam now, which has scared off purists, but the construction is honest for the price. The crepe sole is divisive — it grips well on smooth floors and looks great new, but it darkens fast, picks up dirt, and is impossible to clean perfectly.

Sizing runs roomy. Most people drop half a size. They are not Goodyear welted, they cannot be resoled in a meaningful way, and they will last you maybe two to four years of regular wear before the sole gives up. For $110 that is fair.

I still own a pair of beeswax leather Desert Boots from 2018. They look better now than they did new, which is the highest compliment I can pay them.

### Mid-Entry — Thursday Scout ($199)

Thursday positions itself as affordable Goodyear welted boots and the Scout is their chukka. Three eyelet, leather upper, leather sole with a rubber heel cap. Sizing is generous; many people drop a full size from sneaker size.

Honest take: the leather is decent, not great, and tends to develop creases that are deeper than I would like. The construction is solid for the money, but I would not call this a lifetime boot. A lifetime boot is something you resole multiple times. The Scout will accept maybe one resole before the upper looks tired.

If you are testing whether you actually like chukkas before committing more money, this is the right buy. If you already know you love them, save up.

### Mid-Range — Grant Stone Ottawa ($395)

The Ottawa is my pick at this price. Goodyear welted, leather lined, made in China to a standard that genuinely competes with American brands twice the price. Three eyelets, plain toe, available in calf and chromexcel.

The chromexcel version is the one I reach for. Chromexcel develops the kind of patina that makes people ask where you got your boots. (For background on the leather itself, see the [chromexcel leather guide](/articles/chromexcel-leather-guide).) The shape is elegant — Grant Stone uses their Leo last for the Ottawa, which is a softer, more rounded almond toe than American work brands. It looks dressier than its price suggests.

Resoleable. Replaceable. The pair I bought in 2023 has been to two weddings, three job interviews, and roughly two hundred normal days. They have one set of polish creases I would call beautiful.

### Mid-Premium — Crockett & Jones Tetbury ($600+)

English calfskin, hand-grade construction, Goodyear welted on a leather sole. The Tetbury is essentially a dress chukka — it is what you wear when "smart casual" really means "near a suit."

The dark brown burnished calf version polishes up to a shine. With a proper grey trouser it looks the part at any business occasion short of a black-tie wedding. The leather is firmer than American chromexcel, which means slower break-in but a longer-term appearance of polish.

Honest downside: leather sole on a chukka in wet weather is asking for trouble. C&J offer a Dainite option which I would actually recommend over the leather sole for daily wear.

### Premium — Alden Plain Toe Chukka ($600–$700+)

Alden makes chukkas on multiple lasts. The Barrie last gives a more rounded, casual chukka. The Aberdeen last gives a sleeker, dressier silhouette. Both are made in Massachusetts on Alden's standard 360-degree Goodyear welt.

The chromexcel chukka in colour 8 (a deep oxblood) is genuinely iconic. It has been worn by everyone from architects to musicians for forty years and looks no different now than it did in 1985. That is the whole point of an Alden — they are pricier than they "should" be, but the silhouette is so refined and the leather so well-chosen that the cost-per-wear over a decade is hard to argue with.

Honest downside: Alden has had quality control issues over the last few years. Stitching irregularities, last drift, occasional leather flaws have all been reported. Buy from a stocking dealer who will accept returns and inspect your pair carefully.

## Toe Shape Matters More Than You Think

Plain toe is the chukka default and the right answer ninety percent of the time. A plain toe puts the focus on the leather and the silhouette of the last itself. ## Honest Downsides of Chukkas

I am not going to pretend chukkas are perfect. They are not.

**Limited weather range.** A two- or three-eyelet ankle shaft does almost nothing for snow, slush, or heavy rain. Water gets in the top. If you need a winter boot, look elsewhere.

**Suede is high-maintenance in cities.** Pavement grit, salt in winter, splash from passing buses — suede chukkas in a real city require weekly brushing and a couple of waterproofing treatments per season.

**The silhouette can read as preppy or thin.** On a person with a heavier frame or a baggier wardrobe, a plain toe suede chukka can look mismatched. You want a little weight in the trouser break and the upper body for the proportion to work.

**Cheap chukkas creasing badly.** The vamp on a chukka is short and exposed. Bad leather creases ugly. This is why I would rather spend $395 once than $200 twice.

## FAQ

**Are chukkas good for office wear?**
Yes, in dark brown or black calf. Suede chukkas can work in creative offices but read too casual in finance or law. Stick to closed-corp, polished calf and grey trousers.

**Can I wear chukkas with jeans?**
Yes. Dark wash, slim or straight. Skinny jeans look bad with chukkas — the silhouette of the boot is too elegant to crash into a tapered ankle. Raw denim with a small cuff over snuff suede chukkas is one of the best looks in casual menswear.

**Should I size down?**
Depends on the brand. Clarks run roomy — drop half a size. Thursday runs roomy — drop a full size for some, half for others. Grant Stone, Alden, C&J — go true to your Brannock size and adjust based on lasts. There is no universal answer, but most chukkas will not need to go up.

**How do I break in a chukka?**
Less work than a heavy work boot. Wear them around the house for an hour or two, then short outings, then full days. A leather-lined chukka is usually wearable from day one with mild discomfort that resolves in a week. For more, [how to break in leather boots](/articles/how-to-break-in-leather-boots) covers it in detail.

**Can chukkas be resoled?**
Goodyear welted ones, yes — multiple times if the upper is in good shape. Cemented chukkas like the Clarks Desert can theoretically be resoled but the cost rarely makes sense relative to a new pair.

**Suede or leather for a first chukka?**
If you want versatility for casual wear, suede in sand or snuff. If you want something that handles work too, calf in dark brown. I would not start with chromexcel unless you already know you like the leather.

**Are chukkas appropriate for weddings?**
Casual outdoor weddings, yes. Black-tie, no — wear oxfords. Cocktail or smart-casual indoor weddings, dark calf chukkas are fine and arguably more interesting than a generic black oxford.

**Why do my chukkas always look casual on me?**
Probably the trouser. A chukka with cropped, slim chinos reads casual. A chukka with a proper wool trouser, full break, reads dressy. The boot is the same; the proportion does the heavy lifting.

## What I Would Buy Today

If I lost my entire boot collection and could only repurchase one chukka, it would be the Grant Stone Ottawa in chromexcel. The price-to-quality ratio is the strongest in the market right now, the silhouette is restrained without being boring, and chromexcel patinas in a way that makes a five-year-old pair look better than a new pair.

If I had real money to spend and was buying a forever boot, the Alden plain toe chukka in colour 8 on the Aberdeen last. It is not the most practical chukka in the world. It is the one that will look right in 2030 and 2040 and probably 2050.

A chukka is not the boot that gets photographed most on Instagram. It is the boot that gets worn most by people who have stopped buying boots for the photo.
