## Why Cleaning Suede Is Different

Suede punishes bad technique. Where smooth leather forgives a wrong wipe and a dab of conditioner, suede remembers every mistake — flattened nap, greasy patches, water rings. The good news: once you understand how suede behaves, most damage is preventable and a surprising amount is reversible.

This guide is the one I wish I had when I ruined my first pair of chukkas with a wet paper towel — trial-and-error on real boots, not brush-brand marketing copy.

If you're also caring for smooth leather, start with [how to care for leather boots](/articles/how-to-care-for-leather-boots) — the fundamentals there are completely different from what you'll do to suede.

## Suede vs Nubuck vs Roughout — Clear the Confusion First

Before you clean anything, know what you actually have. The three "fuzzy leathers" look similar but behave differently.

- **Suede**: Made from the *inner* split of the hide (or the flesh side of a thin split). Soft, fine nap, fairly uniform. Least durable of the three. Usually thinner.
- **Nubuck**: Made from the *outer* (grain) side of the hide, then sanded to raise a very fine, dense nap. Feels like velvet. Tougher than suede because the grain layer is still intact underneath.
- **Roughout**: Regular grain leather turned inside out — flesh side worn outside, grain side worn inside. Coarser, uneven nap. Extremely durable. Used in military and work boots.

For a deeper look at roughout specifically, see the [roughout leather deep dive](/articles/roughout-leather-deep-dive).

The cleaning techniques in this guide work on all three, but **nubuck tolerates slightly more aggressive brushing**, and **roughout can take water better than suede can**. When in doubt, treat the material like suede — the gentlest assumption is the safest one.

## Essential Tools — What You Actually Need

You don't need a 12-piece kit. These five items cover 95% of scenarios:

1. **Suede brush (brass + nylon combo)** — Brass side for dried mud and matted nap, nylon for daily dust. Kiwi or Saphir both work; avoid cheap plastic-only brushes that crush the nap.
2. **Crepe rubber eraser (suede bar)** — Lifts grime and scuffs by rolling off in crumbs. One bar lasts years.
3. **Protector spray** — Fluoropolymer or silicone-based. Saphir Super Invulner, Collonil Carbon Pro, and Crep Protect all work.
4. **Nap brush / horsehair brush** — Optional, for restoring direction and softness after cleaning.
5. **Suede shampoo** — Saphir Omni'Nettoyant or Jason Markk. Deep cleans only.

Optional add-ons: white vinegar (salt stains), cornstarch or talcum powder (grease), distilled water in a spray bottle (water spots), old toothbrush (seam detail).

## Daily Maintenance Routine

Treat suede like a suit, not sneakers. A five-minute habit saves hour-long deep cleans later.

**After every wear:**
1. Let the boots rest for 20 minutes to release sweat and ambient moisture.
2. Brush with the nylon side in **one direction** — usually heel toward toe, following the nap.
3. Insert cedar shoe trees. Moisture control matters for suede as much as smooth leather.
4. Store in a ventilated area out of direct sunlight. UV fades suede faster than most smooth leathers.

**Every 4–6 wears:** reapply protector spray.

## Step 1 — Removing Dry Dirt (Brushing Direction Matters)

This is 80% of all suede cleaning. Most "stains" are just ground-in dust.

1. Make sure the boot is completely dry. Never brush wet or damp suede — you'll mat the nap and grind dirt deeper.
2. Use the nylon side of the suede brush first. Sweep in **one consistent direction** with light pressure. Don't scrub back and forth — that crushes the nap.
3. Check the tongue, welt area, and around the eyelets. Dirt hides there.
4. For stubborn spots, switch to the brass side. Brass is aggressive — use short, feathered strokes, not grinding pressure.
5. Finish with a light pass of the nylon side to re-align the nap.

If the dirt is gone but the nap looks "tired" or shiny, skip to the "Restoring Nap" section.

## Step 2 — Removing Wet Mud (Let It Dry First!)

The single most common suede disaster: someone sees mud and immediately attacks it with a wet towel. This pushes mud deeper into the nap and leaves a dark halo that may never come out.

**The correct sequence:**

1. If you can, blot — do not wipe — the biggest blobs off with a dry cloth or paper towel. Press, lift, repeat. No sideways motion.
2. Let the boot dry completely. This takes 6–24 hours depending on humidity. Stuff with newspaper or shoe trees to absorb moisture and hold shape.
3. Once fully dry, the mud will be crusty and flaky. Now brush it off with the brass side of the suede brush.
4. Follow up with the nylon side to smooth out the nap.
5. If a faint stain remains, use the crepe eraser — rub firmly but not aggressively. It will crumble as it lifts grime.

If you jumped straight to a wet cloth and now have a dark water-stained patch, skip to the "Water Spots" section. Don't panic yet.

## Step 3 — Oil and Grease Stains (Cornstarch Method)

Oil is suede's worst enemy because it migrates through the nap and darkens the leather from the flesh side. Act within an hour of the spill if possible.

1. Blot excess oil with a dry paper towel. Do not rub.
2. Cover the entire stain generously with **cornstarch or unscented talcum powder**. Pile it on — think "snow drift," not "light dusting."
3. Leave it overnight (at least 8 hours). The powder draws oil up out of the nap.
4. Brush the powder off thoroughly with the nylon side of the suede brush.
5. If the stain is still visible, repeat the powder treatment. Two or three rounds are normal for larger stains.
6. For a final pass, rub gently with the crepe eraser.

Honest note: oil stains older than a few days may never fully come out. At that point, you're choosing between a faint shadow and a suede recoloring product — see the downsides section at the end.

## Step 4 — Salt Stains (Water + Vinegar)

Winter's signature damage: white crusty rings from road salt. Salt is corrosive to leather and will literally eat fibers if left alone, so don't ignore these.

1. Mix **equal parts white vinegar and distilled water** in a small bowl.
2. Dampen — not soak — a clean white cloth with the solution.
3. Dab the salt ring gently. Work from the outside of the ring inward.
4. Repeat with a fresh dampened cloth to rinse.
5. Stuff the boot with newspaper, let it dry completely at room temperature (no heat).
6. Once dry, brush to restore the nap.

For heavy winter use, preventative waterproofing is essential. See [how to waterproof leather boots](/articles/how-to-waterproof-leather-boots) for broader context — the principles apply to suede with a different product choice (suede-safe fluoropolymer spray instead of wax).

## Step 5 — Water Spots (Whole-Boot Wet Method)

If part of your boot got wet and dried with a visible ring or darker patch, the fix sounds terrifying but works.

1. Using a spray bottle filled with **distilled water**, lightly and evenly mist the **entire boot** — not just the stain. The goal is uniform dampness across the whole surface.
2. Do not soak. Stop as soon as the suede looks evenly darkened.
3. Stuff with newspaper or shoe trees and let the boots air-dry away from heat, for 24 hours.
4. When fully dry, brush vigorously to restore nap.
5. The water ring will have vanished because the whole boot now dried together.

Use tap water only if you don't have distilled; mineral content can leave faint deposits but is usually acceptable.

## Step 6 — Deep Cleaning with Suede Shampoo

Reserve this for seasonal cleans or genuinely dirty boots. Shampoo strips some color; you may need to recolor afterward.

1. Brush the boots thoroughly to remove loose dirt.
2. Wet a sponge with lukewarm water. Apply a small amount of suede shampoo and work up a light foam on the sponge.
3. Apply the foam — not wet shampoo — to the suede in small circles. Cover the entire boot to avoid water rings.
4. Wipe off residue with a clean damp cloth.
5. Stuff with newspaper, dry for 24–48 hours.
6. Brush to restore nap.
7. Reapply protector spray once fully dry.

Expect the suede to look slightly duller after shampooing. That's normal — brushing and protector spray will bring the look back.

## Restoring Matted Nap

After heavy cleaning or simply months of wear, the nap can lie flat and look shiny or "leathery." This is fixable.

**Method 1 — Brass brush:** Short, feathered strokes with the brass bristles lift flattened fibers. Don't grind.

**Method 2 — Steam:** Hold a kettle or steam iron (no contact) about 15 cm away from the suede for 5–10 seconds. The warm moisture plumps the fibers. Immediately brush while damp to lift the nap.

**Method 3 — Crepe eraser:** For small shiny patches, a firm rub with the crepe eraser abrades the surface just enough to raise the nap.

Stop as soon as the nap looks uniform — overworking leaves bald spots.

## Protector Sprays — When and How Often

Protector spray is non-negotiable if you want your suede to survive more than a season.

**When to apply:**
- Before the first wear — always. Brand new suede goes on the shelf, not on your feet, until it's been sprayed.
- After every deep clean or shampoo.
- Every 4–6 wears during regular use.
- Before any forecasted rain or snow.

**How to apply:**
1. Brush the boots clean and dry.
2. Spray in a well-ventilated area (outdoors ideally). Hold the can 20–30 cm away.
3. Apply two light coats, letting each dry 10–15 minutes.
4. Do not oversaturate — heavy spray can darken suede temporarily or permanently.
5. Wait 24 hours before wearing.

## What NOT to Do — A List of Suede Killers

These mistakes cause most of the "ruined suede" horror stories.

- **Don't use leather conditioner on suede.** Conditioners that work wonders on smooth leather saturate suede fibers with oil and permanently darken them. Suede is finished, not conditioned.
- **Don't scrub with water.** Wet scrubbing mats the nap and spreads stains. Always dry first, then brush.
- **Don't use heat to dry wet suede.** Hair dryers, radiators, and direct sun harden the fibers and can shrink the boot. Air-dry at room temperature.
- **Don't use saddle soap.** It's designed for smooth leather and will strip and stiffen suede.
- **Don't apply protector to dirty suede.** You'll seal dirt into the nap.
- **Don't brush wet suede aggressively.** Brush only when dry unless you're specifically using the whole-boot wet method.
- **Don't store suede in plastic.** Trapped moisture causes mildew. Use breathable cloth bags.
- **Don't use household cleaners** (dish soap, laundry detergent, carpet cleaner) — they're pH-wrong and strip color.

## Honest Downsides — Suede Is High Maintenance

The part most guides leave out.

**Suede demands 3–5x the effort of smooth leather.** Even with perfect care. If you want low-effort footwear, suede is the wrong choice.

**Some stains are permanent.** Deep oil, old mud that was wet-scrubbed, ink, and certain road chemicals leave lasting shadows. Recoloring dye can disguise them but won't remove them.

**Recoloring has limits.** Dye matches work best on darker suedes (navy, brown, charcoal). Light colors (sand, tan, pale grey) are nearly impossible to restore invisibly — dye bleeds between old and new areas.

**Rain is always a risk.** Even protected suede can water-spot in heavy rain. If you live somewhere wet, keep suede for dry days and rotate in [chromexcel leather](/articles/chromexcel-leather-guide) or other oil-tanned options for daily wear.

**Break-in is less forgiving.** Because suede is thinner, it breaks in faster but mistakes leave permanent marks. See [how to break in leather boots](/articles/how-to-break-in-leather-boots) for general principles.

**Suede doesn't patina like smooth leather.** It fades unevenly and the "character" it develops is less universally admired. If patina is your thing, read the [patina and aging guide](/articles/patina-and-aging-guide) — smooth leather is the better canvas.

None of this means don't buy suede. It means go in with eyes open.

## FAQ

### Can you machine wash suede boots?
Absolutely not. Machine washing will destroy the glue in the construction, warp the shape, and permanently flatten the nap. Even on a gentle cycle with cold water, the damage is severe. Clean suede by hand only.

### Will suede be ruined in the rain?
Unprotected suede is highly vulnerable to rain. With a good protector spray applied before wear, suede can survive light rain with minimal damage. Heavy rain will still cause water spots — use the whole-boot wet method afterward to even out the color.

### How often should I protect my suede boots?
Reapply protector spray every 4–6 wears during regular use, before forecasted bad weather, and after every deep clean. New boots should always get two coats before their first wear.

### Can I use a pencil eraser on suede?
A clean white rubber eraser works in a pinch for tiny scuffs, but a dedicated crepe rubber suede bar is much better. Pencil erasers can leave colored residue and are too small to cover meaningful areas.

### What if my suede turns shiny or smooth from wear?
The nap has been matted down. Use a brass-bristle suede brush with short, feathered strokes to raise the fibers. Light steam (from a kettle, held at distance) followed by brushing works well for stubborn shiny patches.

### Is it okay to wear suede in winter?
Yes, but you're signing up for serious maintenance. Salt stains are the biggest threat. Apply protector spray aggressively, brush off salt residue the same day, and treat any salt rings with the vinegar solution immediately. Many owners rotate suede out of the winter lineup entirely.

### Can I dye my suede boots a different color?
You can darken them (tan to brown, brown to dark brown), but you cannot lighten them. Dye matching is more forgiving on dark colors. Test on a hidden area like the inside of the tongue before committing.

### Why does my suede have dark patches after cleaning?
Usually because water dried unevenly. Use the whole-boot wet method — mist the entire boot with distilled water, then let it air-dry as a single unit. The patches typically disappear.

### How long do suede boots last with proper care?
A well-made suede boot (welted construction, quality suede) can last 8–15 years with proper care — less than smooth leather in harsh conditions, but comparable in gentle use. The uppers often outlast several resolings.

### Is nubuck easier to care for than suede?
Slightly. Nubuck's nap is denser and sits on top of an intact grain layer, so it resists water and dirt marginally better. The cleaning techniques are identical.

## Final Thoughts

Suede isn't difficult to care for — it's *specific*. The techniques are simple once you know them, but applying the wrong one (wet cloth on fresh mud, conditioner meant for smooth leather, heat drying) causes damage that's often permanent.

Build the daily habit: brush after every wear, protect every few wears, act on stains before they set. Do that and your suede boots look good for a decade. Skip it and they'll look rough within a season.

Suede wins on looks and comfort; smooth leather wins on durability and ease. Both can last a lifetime if you respect what they need.
