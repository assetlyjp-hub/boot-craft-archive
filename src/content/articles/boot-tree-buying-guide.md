The first pair of $400 boots I ever bought, I didn't put trees in for the first six months. I figured trees were one of those upsell items — $40 hunks of wood the salesman pushes after you've committed to the boots themselves. By month seven I had a deep crack across the vamp where the leather had folded too hard while sitting in my closet.

That mistake cost me a $150 recrafting bill. A pair of Allen Edmonds lasted boot trees would have cost $80. I have not skipped boot trees on a single pair since.

This guide covers what boot trees actually do (and what they don't), the real difference between cedar and plain wood, why full-foot trees are worth the upcharge over spring-loaded ones, and the specific brands worth your money. There's also an honest section on when you can skip trees entirely — because not every boot in your closet needs one.

## What boot trees actually do

Three things, in order of importance:

**1. They preserve the shape of the upper.** Leather has memory. After you walk in a boot, the upper folds at the vamp (the flex point across the toes), and that crease starts to set. Without a tree, the boot dries in that creased position, and over time the crease becomes a permanent fold. With a tree, the leather dries pulled smooth across the last shape, and the creases relax.

**2. They absorb moisture.** This is where cedar matters. Your foot puts off about a quarter cup of moisture per day during normal wear, more in summer or during exertion. That moisture saturates the leather lining and the cork insole. Cedar is hygroscopic — it pulls moisture out of surrounding materials. Plain wood (beech, maple) does this too, but less aggressively.

**3. They prevent the curl-up of the toe.** When boots dry without trees, the toe tends to curl upward as the leather tightens. You've seen it on neglected work boots — that "elf shoe" look. A properly fitted tree holds the toe down and forward in its original shape.

What boot trees *don't* do:
- They don't condition the leather (you still need conditioner)
- They don't prevent all creasing (creases are inevitable; trees minimize their depth)
- They don't fix existing deep creases (they slow further damage but won't reverse)
- They don't waterproof or protect against weather

## Cedar vs plain wood: the actual difference

This is the question every guide gets wrong, so let me be specific. Both cedar and plain wood (typically beech, maple, or birch) work fine for shape preservation. The shape work is done by the tree's structure and tension, not the wood species.

The difference is moisture absorption and odor.

**Cedar** — specifically aromatic Eastern Red Cedar (Juniperus virginiana) — has open pores and natural oils that pull moisture out of leather more effectively than dense hardwoods. It also smells distinctly of cedar, which most people enjoy and which has mild antimicrobial properties (it's why cedar closets work for moth deterrence).

**Plain hardwood** — usually European beech for the European brands, hard maple for North American — is denser and absorbs less moisture. It's also more dimensionally stable, meaning it doesn't expand and contract as much with humidity changes. This is why most heritage shoemakers (Alden, Allen Edmonds, John Lobb) use beech for their lasted trees: it holds the boot's shape more precisely over decades.

**My honest take:** for boots you wear regularly in temperate climates, cedar is the better practical choice because moisture management matters more than micro-precision in shape. For boots you wear less often, or in dry climates, beech is fine and the lasted shape is worth the upcharge.

The cedar oil thing is overrated. After 6-12 months the surface oil is gone and the cedar smell fades dramatically. People online recommend sanding the trees with fine-grit sandpaper to "refresh" the cedar — this works, but it's marginal. A drop of cedar oil from the hardware store is more effective if the smell genuinely matters to you.

## Full-foot vs split/spring-loaded: not even close

There are roughly three configurations of boot tree on the market:

**Full-foot lasted trees.** A solid, single piece of wood (or two pieces hinged at the heel) shaped to match a specific last. These mimic the actual shape of the boot's last and provide even pressure across the entire upper. The best ones are made specifically for a particular shoe model — Alden's lasted trees are shaped to match the Aberdeen, Plaza, Barrie, etc. lasts.

**Split-toe trees with a spring tube.** Two pieces — a heel block and a toe block — connected by a coil spring or telescoping shaft. These adjust to fit a range of shoe sizes, which is why retailers love them. The spring provides forward tension, but the tree doesn't fill the boot the way a lasted tree does. Decent for shape preservation, less ideal.

**Single-piece spring trees (the cheap kind).** A single block of wood with a separate tension spring that pushes against the heel. These are common at $15-25. They preserve some forward shape but provide essentially no support for the boot's upper. Better than nothing, worse than anything else.

The price spread is real: a lasted Alden tree runs $90-130 per pair, a quality split-toe Woodlore is $40-60, a basic spring tree is $15-20. Is the lasted tree worth six times the basic? For a $700 boot, yes. For a $200 boot, the split-toe is the right call. For a $90 work boot you wear in mud, even a basic spring tree is overkill — see the "when boot trees don't matter" section below.

## Brand recommendations

### Allen Edmonds Lasted Boot Trees ($85)

These are the standard recommendation in the heritage boot community for good reason. American hard maple, lasted to fit Allen Edmonds' last shapes, with a matte finish that doesn't transfer color to the leather. They fit AE boots perfectly and work well for any boot built on a similar last shape (most American-style boots).

The split design opens 90 degrees, which makes them easy to insert without scraping the boot's interior. The grain finish has a slight tooth that grips the heel cup and doesn't slip out when you lift the boot.

I have eight pairs of these in rotation. They've outlasted two pairs of boots so far.

### Stratton Boot Trees ($80-100)

Stratton is the maker behind a lot of premium-brand trees — they OEM for several boot companies under different labels. Buying direct gets you the same product at a better price. American-made cedar, hand-shaped, with a turn-knob heel block that adjusts vertical pressure.

I prefer Stratton for boots taller than 6 inches. The shaft support is better than Allen Edmonds because Stratton offers a tall-shaft model specifically for 8-inch and taller boots.

### Woodlore Combination Trees ($45)

The value pick. Aromatic cedar, split-toe with a spring tube, made in Vermont. They don't last-match like the AE trees, but for boots in the $200-400 range they're the right balance of price and function.

Woodlore comes in size ranges (8.5-9.5, 10-10.5, etc.) so make sure you order the right one. They run slightly small — if you're between sizes go up.

### Saphir Cedar Boot Trees ($75)

French-made cedar with a refined finish. The wood is more carefully selected than most American cedar trees — fewer knots, more consistent color. The shape is more European-foot-shaped (slightly narrower in the toe, more curved at the instep), which fits Crockett & Jones, John Lobb, and similar English boots better than Allen Edmonds trees do.

If your collection is European-leaning, these are worth the upcharge over Woodlore.

### Sleipnir Beech Boot Trees ($110-150)

For the John Lobb, Edward Green, and Vass crowd. Czech-made from European beech, hand-finished, with a precision that matches actual lasted production. Not cheap, not necessary unless you have boots in this tier — but if you do, the dimensional precision matters.

## How many trees do you actually need?

The general rule is one pair of trees per pair of boots, but that's a sales pitch. The real rule is one pair of trees per pair of boots in active rotation.

Your daily-wear boots need trees inserted within minutes of taking them off so the leather dries in the correct shape. This means a 1:1 ratio for boots you wear regularly.

Boots you wear once a month or less? You can get away with sharing trees if the boots are similar sizes and shapes. I rotate one set of Stratton trees between three pairs of dressy boots that I wear less frequently. The trees go into whichever pair I just took off, sit overnight, then move to whichever pair I wore next. It's not optimal but it works.

The rotation math for someone with a real collection:
- 5 pairs in regular rotation = 5 pairs of trees
- 3 pairs occasional wear = 1-2 shared sets of trees
- Total: 6-7 pairs of trees for 8 pairs of boots

Don't buy trees you'll never use. Empty trees stored in a drawer are wasted money.

## When boot trees DON'T matter

There are categories of boot where I think trees are genuinely unnecessary or low-priority:

**Heavy work boots used for actual work.** Red Wing 4585s on a job site, White's Smokejumpers in the woods — these boots are designed to flex, get wet, dry hard, and be replaced every few years. The aesthetic of deep creases is part of the look. Tree money is better spent on a second pair for rotation.

**Casual rotation boots worn fewer than 5 times a year.** If you're putting trees into a pair of fashion-forward Chelsea boots that you wear three times annually, the trees aren't doing meaningful work. The leather isn't getting saturated with sweat between wears.

**Boots with synthetic uppers or heavy rubber components.** Trees are for shaping leather. Sorel pacs, Bogs, rubberized hiking boots — none of these benefit from a tree.

**Brand-new boots in their boxes you haven't worn yet.** Don't buy trees for boots you don't own yet. The boots come pre-shaped on the last from the factory. They don't need trees until they've been worn and the leather has started to take on your foot's shape.

The honest summary: trees matter most for fine leather boots in regular rotation. They matter least for work boots, infrequent wears, and synthetic-heavy footwear.

## How to use trees correctly

Most people insert trees and walk away. There's a slightly better method:

1. **Wait 5-15 minutes after taking the boots off** before inserting trees. This lets the surface moisture evaporate so the trees absorb more deeply.

2. **Wipe down the upper with a soft cloth** before inserting trees. Removes surface dust and prevents grit from getting trapped between the tree and the inner lining.

3. **Insert the toe block first**, push it forward into the toe box, then engage the heel block. For lasted trees, twist slightly to seat the tree against the heel cup.

4. **Leave trees in for at least 24 hours**, ideally until the next wear. The first 6 hours do most of the moisture work, but the shape preservation continues for as long as the tree is in.

5. **For long storage** (off-season pairs), leave trees in continuously. Pull boots out monthly to inspect, but the trees stay home.

This pairs well with a proper [boot care schedule](/articles/boot-care-schedule). Trees do the day-to-day work; conditioning every 8-12 weeks does the leather work.

## Honest downsides of boot trees

To balance the recommendation: there are real downsides.

**They're expensive.** $80-130 per pair adds up fast across a collection. For someone building their first nice boot setup, the trees can feel like a bigger investment than expected.

**They take up storage space.** A boot with a tree inside takes up about 30% more shelf space than a boot without. Closet space matters.

**The wrong tree can damage a boot.** A tree that's too big can stretch the heel counter or vamp permanently. A tree that's too small flops around and provides minimal benefit. Sizing matters — and split-toe trees in size ranges are better at fitting flexibly than rigid lasted trees.

**Cedar can dry out leather over very long periods.** This is genuinely contested in the community, but I've seen it happen on boots stored with cedar trees in heated dry winters for multiple years without conditioning. Cedar pulls moisture out of leather, and leather needs *some* moisture to stay supple. Pair cedar tree storage with regular conditioning. See [how to care for leather boots](/articles/how-to-care-for-leather-boots).

## FAQ

**Are cedar trees actually better than plain wood?**
For moisture absorption, yes. For shape preservation, the wood doesn't matter — the structure does. If you live somewhere humid or sweat a lot, cedar earns its keep. If you live in a dry climate, plain hardwood is fine.

**Can I use one set of trees for multiple pairs of boots?**
Yes, if the pairs are similar sizes and you're not wearing all of them daily. Rotate trees between less-worn pairs. Daily-wear boots should each have their own trees.

**Do trees really prevent creasing?**
They minimize creasing. They don't eliminate it. A crease at the vamp is inevitable on any boot you walk in — leather flexes there. Trees keep the crease shallow rather than deep, and prevent secondary creasing across the toe box.

**How often should I sand my cedar trees to refresh them?**
Once a year is plenty. Light sanding with 220-grit paper exposes fresh cedar oils. Don't oversand — you're trying to remove the oxidized surface layer, not reshape the tree.

**Can I make my own boot trees?**
You can. People have used rolled-up newspaper, wadded socks, even foam blocks. They preserve shape (poorly) but don't absorb moisture. For a $200 boot, real trees are worth it. For a $50 boot you don't care about long-term, newspaper is fine.

**Do I need trees for boots I'm [breaking in](/articles/how-to-break-in-leather-boots)?**
Yes — even more so. Break-in is when leather is most actively reshaping, and trees ensure it's reshaping in the right direction (matching your foot in wear, returning to the last shape at rest). Skipping trees during break-in is how boots end up permanently distorted.

**What about trees for [resoled boots](/articles/resoling-guide)?**
Same as new boots. Recrafting includes a re-lasting step where the upper is reshaped on its original last. Trees maintain that shape through the new leather's settling period.

**Do trees affect [patina development](/articles/patina-and-aging-guide)?**
Indirectly. Trees keep leather smoother, which lets light reflect more evenly across the surface. The result is a more uniform patina rather than a heavily creased, "character" patina. Both look good — trees push toward the smoother end of that spectrum.

## What I'd buy starting over

For someone building their first boot collection (3 pairs total), my recommendation is:
1. Two pairs of Woodlore Combination Trees ($90 total)
2. One pair of Allen Edmonds Lasted Trees ($85) for the dressiest boot

Total: about $175 for trees that handle a starter rotation properly.

For someone with 6+ pairs of higher-end boots, scale up to lasted trees for the regular rotation and Stratton or Saphir for the occasional pairs. Plan to spend roughly 15-20% of your boot budget on trees if you want them done right.

The big lesson: trees aren't an upsell, they're insurance. The cost of a quality tree is a fraction of a recrafting fee, and recrafting only saves a boot once or twice before the upper is too far gone. Good trees, used consistently, are how a $400 pair of boots becomes a 15-year investment instead of a 5-year wardrobe item.

Pair the trees with a regular conditioning routine and proper [waterproofing](/articles/how-to-waterproof-leather-boots), and you've covered 90% of what determines whether a boot lives or dies.
